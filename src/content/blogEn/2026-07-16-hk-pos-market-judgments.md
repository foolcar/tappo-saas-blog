> **Editor's note:** Last month I spent two weeks tearing down 8 major Hong Kong POS systems and wrote a [comparison piece](/en/blog/2026-07-02-hk-pos-battle-2026/). That one was a buyer's guide. This one is the private notebook I kept as someone who actually builds restaurant SaaS. Full disclosure: I'm the founder of Tappo, so my bias is on the table. Read with skepticism.

## Tearing Down 8 Systems Taught Me the Opposite of What I Expected

I went in thinking two weeks of ripping apart Hong Kong's POS landscape would produce a clean "who wins" scorecard.

It did the opposite. The strongest feeling I walked away with wasn't "brand X is strong" — it was that **this market has no shortage of POS systems, but a serious shortage of anyone admitting what they can't do.**

Seven of the eight pitch "most features," "most local," "best service." None say "here's where we genuinely can't help you."

This isn't a scorecard. It's 4 judgments from someone inside the industry.

---

## Judgment 1: The Monthly Fee Is a Tax, Not a Service

Seven of eight charge a monthly fee, from HK$188 to HK$888. Only Tappo (mine) charges per order.

I used to think monthly SaaS pricing was just how things work. Tearing down competitors showed me the monthly fee is fundamentally a **lock-in, not a service.**

Why? Because it ignores usage. A corner shop doing 50 orders a day pays the same as a chain doing 500. The small shop suffocates under the fee; the chain stopped noticing it months ago.

More importantly: the monthly fee removes the vendor's incentive to help you grow. Your revenue goes up or down — the fee stays. The POS provider's income is decoupled from your store's performance.

That's exactly why we built Tappo on per-order pricing — **to tie our revenue to the owner's revenue.** One more order, one more credit. No orders, no charge. Not a philosophy. Alignment of interest.

(To be fair, this cuts both ways — when you're slow, we eat too. But I'd rather stand on the owner's side.)

---

## Judgment 2: Feature Bloat Is Fake Demand. Subtraction Is the Moat.

By the third system I noticed a pattern: everyone races to list "do I have KDS / membership / inventory." The feature checklist grows longer.

But having watched thousands of restaurants through Cando (our delivery-management arm), **most restaurants use less than 20% of a POS daily.**

The other 80%? The owner doesn't know it exists, wouldn't use it if they did, or opened it once and never again.

Here's the catch: more features means more complexity, longer training, higher error rates. A cha chaan teng staff member — do you want them to memorize 15 button logics, or 5?

So I believe more and more: **the real moat isn't "I have everything," it's "I help you stop thinking about the system."**

It's why iCHEF's interface genuinely feels good to me — not the most features, but ordering reduced to something thoughtless. That restraint is far harder than piling on checkboxes.

---

## Judgment 3: Delivery Integration Is Redrawing the POS Map

This is the judgment that changed my mind the most.

I assumed the core of POS was in-store checkout. Hong Kong isn't that. Delivery is 30%+ of F&B revenue here — over half for some shops.

A POS that doesn't integrate Deliveroo, Foodpanda, and Keeta well is shutting 30% of the business out the door.

Tearing them down made it clear: **Eats365 leads the field here by a length.** Native integration with all three, orders auto-routed to one backend, no app-switching for staff. For delivery-heavy shops, that alone pays for itself.

Real takeaway: delivery integration isn't a "nice-to-have" anymore. It's the **foundation** of what a good POS is. Whoever builds this solid redraws the map.

(This is exactly where Tappo is catching up — the MOS ecosystem is wiring up Minitable, Cando, and the delivery platforms. But I'll be honest: Eats365 is currently ahead of us here.)

---

## Judgment 4: Small Shops Are the Ignored 80%

Last judgment, and it's a slightly uncomfortable one.

Of the eight, at least five have their eyes on mid-to-large chains. Higher ARPU, bigger contracts, prettier case studies.

But what is Hong Kong? Streets of dai pai dong, bing sutt, tiny family shops. Those are the 80% by count.

Their pain is concrete: tight budget, low tech literacy, hard to hire, hate long contracts. HK$888/month is astronomical to them.

Result? **The 80% with the most need are the 80% least served by vendors.**

The more I sit with this, the more I see opportunity, not complaint. A system with zero monthly fee, runs on the tablet you already own, set up in 30 minutes, charges only per order — that's built for the 80%.

This loops back to Tappo. I won't pretend we "spotted a blue ocean" — more accurately, we started from small-shop needs, then realized most players never seriously served them.

---

## Four Judgments, One Line

If I had to compress all four into one sentence:

**The next decade of Hong Kong's POS market won't belong to the one with the most features — it'll belong to the one most willing to align its interest with the owner's.**

The monthly-fee model split the vendor and the owner apart. Whoever re-binds them wins.

I won't claim Tappo will be that one. But we're at least walking that direction on purpose.
